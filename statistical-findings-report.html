Executive summary

This report presents an advanced investigation of cultural tastes, culinary authenticity, and social distinction using survey data on food preferences, dining habits, and demographic profiles. Building upon a Multiple Factor Analysis (MFA), we examine how individuals navigate a multidimensional “taste space” and how their locations in this space are structured by socioeconomic resources, gender, race/ethnicity, and political ideology.

By leveraging advanced multivariate statistical techniques—including Multivariate Analysis of Variance (MANOVA), Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA), and Hierarchical Clustering on Principal Components (HCPC)—we demonstrate that:

  1. Culinary taste is structured by three distinct dimensions: a socioeconomic “Fancy Taste” axis, an educational/generational “Common Taste” axis, and a highly political and secular “Foodie Taste” axis.
  2. Social position is a highly significant joint predictor of tastes: MANOVA reveals that race/ethnicity, gender, and political ideology exert highly significant, independent multivariate effects on taste positioning.
  3. Ideology organizes food aesthetics: LDA shows that political ideology is primarily sorted along the “Foodie Taste” dimension, which acts as a powerful cultural divider between Liberals and Conservatives.
  4. Three distinct culinary subcultures exist: HCPC partitions respondents into Traditional/Cautious, Local/Socioeconomic, and Omnivorous/High-End subcultures, whose membership probabilities exhibit stark life-course and ideological trajectories.

Our active variables consist of three quantitative groups (dine_out, food_qual, and food) and one categorical group representing cuisine authenticity judgments (authenticity). Key regression findings

  1. Fancy Taste (Dim. 1): Strongly structured by socioeconomic resources. Higher education (\(b = 0.155\), \(p < 0.001\)), childhood arts exposure (\(b = 0.119\), \(p < 0.001\)), household income (\(b = 0.022\), \(p < 0.05\)), and larger cities (\(b = 0.103\), \(p < 0.001\)) significantly increase fancy taste scores.   3. Foodie Taste (Dim. 3): Strongly structured by politics and secularism. Being a Liberal significantly increases foodie taste scores (\(b = 0.427\), \(p < 0.001\)) compared to Moderates, while high religious attendance significantly reduces them (\(b = -0.054\), \(p < 0.01\)). Additionally, older respondents (\(b = 0.009\), \(p < 0.001\)) and those with high childhood arts exposure (\(b = 0.054\), \(p < 0.01\)) hold higher foodie taste scores.

Post-hoc pairwise comparisons for race/ethnicity

To identify which specific race/ethnicity groups differ significantly in their multidimensional taste centroids, we perform pairwise MANOVAs between all combinations of groups, adjusting for multiple comparisons using the Bonferroni method. We then follow up with univariate Tukey’s Honestly Significant Difference (HSD) tests to determine which specific taste dimensions drive these differences.

Execute post-hoc pairwise comparisons 
# 1. Pairwise MANOVA
levels_race <- levels(mfa.dat$race.f)
pairs <- combn(levels_race, 2)

results <- list()
for (i in 1:ncol(pairs)) {
  g1 <- pairs[1, i]
  g2 <- pairs[2, i]
  
  sub_dat <- mfa.dat |> filter(race.f %in% c(g1, g2))
  fit <- manova(cbind(Dim.1, Dim.2, Dim.3) ~ race.f, data = sub_dat)
  summary_fit <- summary(fit, test = "Pillai")
  
  pillai <- summary_fit$stats[1, "Pillai"]
  p_val <- summary_fit$stats[1, "Pr(>F)"]
  
  results[[i]] <- data.frame(
    Comparison = paste(g1, "vs.", g2),
    Pillai = pillai,
    p_value = p_val
  )
}

pairwise_df <- do.call(rbind, results) |> 
  mutate(p_adj_bonferroni = p.adjust(p_value, method = "bonferroni")) |> 
  arrange(p_value)

Food subcultures and life course trends (HCPC)

We use Hierarchical Clustering on Principal Components (HCPC) to group individuals into homogenous “taste profiles” or cultural food subcultures.

Execute HCPC

The hierarchical algorithm identifies 3 optimal clusters: 1. Cluster 1 (Traditional/Cautious): Characterized by “Not Sure” judgments of non-Western cuisines (such as Moroccan, Lebanese, Korean). This cluster is dominated by political Moderates and white respondents. 2. Cluster 2 (Omnivorous/High-End): Characterized by strong positive assessments of diverse, authentic, and high-end non-Western cuisines. 3. Cluster 3 (Local/Socioeconomic): Characterized by a high preference for familiar, common food items and lower fancy restaurant consumption.

Key cluster probability trends

The faceted predicted probability plot reveals stark differences: * Cluster 1 (Traditional/Cautious): The probability of joining this cautious cluster strongly increases with age for all groups, but is consistently highest for Conservatives and Moderates, reaching nearly 70% for older Conservatives. * Cluster 2 (Omnivorous/High-End): Highly concentrated among younger Liberals (probability starts above 60% for young Liberals and declines steeply with age). Younger Conservatives have a much lower probability (~25%) of belonging to this high-end omnivore subculture. * Cluster 3 (Local/Socioeconomic): Remains relatively stable or slightly declining across age groups, but shows that Moderates and Conservatives of all ages are consistently more aligned with local/common tastes than their Liberal counterparts.
