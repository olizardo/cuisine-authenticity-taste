1 Introduction and Theoretical Framework

Cultural tastes and consumption practices are central pillars of social stratification, serving as primary vehicles for the display of status, cultural capital, and symbolic boundaries. Drawing on the foundational work of Pierre Bourdieu and subsequent expansions in the sociology of culture, this study investigates how culinary tastes and cuisine authenticity judgments are organized in a multidimensional “taste space.”

Instead of treating tastes as isolated preferences, we employ a geometric data analysis (GDA) framework. This approach assumes that individual preferences are organized in relation to one another and structured by overarching, latent axes of evaluation. To capture this complexity, we use Multiple Factor Analysis (MFA). Unlike standard Principal Component Analysis (PCA), which treats all variables uniformly, MFA groups variables into pre-defined survey blocks (e.g., dining frequency, culinary flavor profiles, and cuisine authenticity judgments). It balances the influence of these different blocks so that no single set of questions dominates the first dimension, while preserving the metric structures of the continuous variables.

Following the geometric extraction of these dimensions, we conduct a comprehensive, multi-step analytical sequence: 1. Descriptive & Bivariate Audit: Summarizing sample characteristics and mapping the dense bivariate correlations across our culinary indicators. 2. Dimensional Extraction (MFA): Constructing the multidimensional taste space and mapping active variable correlations. 3. Formal Multivariate Analysis of Variance (MANOVA): Testing the joint distribution of centroids across the complete socio-demographic space, exploring key structural and ideological interactions. 4. Pairwise Centroid Projections with 95% SE Ellipses: Mapping joint uncertainty space for primary demographic cleavages. 5. Hierarchical Clustering (HCPC): Segmenting respondents into distinct culinary subcultures. 6. Multinomial Logistic Modeling & Faceted Probability Curves: Tracing the life-course and ideological trajectories of subculture membership.

4 Multiple Factor Analysis (MFA)

We perform a Multiple Factor Analysis (MFA) on our standardized culinary taste active indicators, with socio-demographics and political ideologies included as supplementary variables to map our multidimensional taste space. The active variables are grouped into four natural survey blocks: dining frequency, general taste preferences, individual food preferences, and cuisine authenticity judgments. MFA balances the influence of these blocks so that no single set of questions dominates the first dimension.

Execute Multiple Factor Analysis 
mfa.res <- MFA(
    base = dat,
    group = c(2, 6, 6, 15, 7, 12),
    type = c("s", "s", "s", "s", "s", "n"),

    num.group.sup = c(5, 6),
    ncp = 5,

7 Hierarchical Clustering (HCPC)

To identify distinct culinary subcultures, we apply Hierarchical Clustering on Principal Components (HCPC) to the first three MFA dimensions using Ward’s agglomerative linkage.

We retain 3 optimal clusters based on both statistical and substantive criteria: 1. Inertia Partitioning (The Elbow Rule): The choice of 3 clusters is mathematically optimal under Ward’s criterion. When the clustering algorithm is run with automatic selection (nb.clust = -1), it recommends exactly 3 clusters. This partition maximizes the ratio of between-cluster inertia to total inertia, representing the point of maximum curvature (the “elbow”) in the hierarchical tree. Merging from 3 to 2 clusters causes a major, statistically unacceptable spike in within-cluster inertia, whereas splitting from 3 to 4 clusters yields negligible gains in variance explained. 2. Substantive Sociological Interpretability: A 3-cluster solution yields highly distinct, cohesive, and theoretically meaningful culinary subcultures: - Everyday Convenience Seekers (favoring fast food, familiar flavors, and large portions). - Traditional/Conservative Homebodies (preferring home-prepared heritage cuisines and rejecting fast-food convenience). - Culinary Explorers / High-End Enthusiasts (high-frequency fine dining, favoring professional chef preparation, exotic flavors, and small portion sizes). Expanding the partition to 4 or more clusters merely splits these primary subcultures into smaller, unstable subgroups without adding further explanatory power or structural clarity.

8.1 Plot 9: Highly Predictive Variables of Cluster Membership

We ran a Type III Analysis of Deviance (Wald Chi-square tests) on our multinomial model to identify which variables are most predictive of taste subculture membership. Four structural and cultural variables emerged as highly significant (\(p < 0.01\)): Education Level, Religiosity, City Size, and Childhood Arts Exposure. We plot the predicted probabilities for these four variables below, ordered structurally (e.g., from low to high education), holding all other covariates constant at their baseline profiles.
